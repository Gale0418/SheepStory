# Technical Explanation Voice

## Purpose

This reference helps the skill write competent first-person or close-POV technical prose: practical reasoning, crisis humor, worked calculations, engineering constraints, and scientific explanation embedded in character voice.

The target is not to reproduce any specific passage, book, franchise, translation, setting, plot, character, or distinctive author voice. The target is a general craft pattern:

```text
Explain the science because the character needs it to survive, decide, build, repair, argue, or understand what went wrong.
```

## Core Rule

Technical explanation must be motivated by scene pressure.

Do not add a lecture because the author wants to show research. Add explanation because the character is solving a problem, defending a decision, noticing an anomaly, improvising, or trying not to die.

## Voice Profile

Useful traits:

- practical, not ceremonial
- direct first-person or close-POV reasoning
- dry humor under pressure
- self-correction and calculation steps
- concrete units, materials, tolerances, temperatures, timings, mass, distance, acceleration, latency, oxygen, heat, fuel, signal delay
- small jokes that reveal fear without stopping the scene
- confidence limited by known unknowns
- explanation that changes the next action

Avoid:

- textbook tone
- omniscient lecture
- decorative jargon
- equations with no story consequence
- too-perfect genius monologue
- generic science-flavored fog
- infodump outside character need

## Worked Reasoning Pattern

When a character reasons through a technical problem, use this shape:

```markdown
### Technical Reasoning Beat

- Immediate problem:
- Observed clue:
- Known rule / remembered principle:
- Quick estimate:
- Uncertainty / margin:
- Decision:
- Risk if wrong:
- Human reaction:
```

The explanation should move from observation to action.

## Example Directions, Not Copy Targets

Do not copy these exact phrasings into story prose. Use them as abstract directions.

### Bad

```text
The alloy had a high melting point, so it would remain intact longer than the surrounding structure.
```

### Better Direction

```text
Have the POV character compare melting points, remember that thickness does not matter until the material reaches its phase-change temperature, choose a safety margin, then use a simple kitchen analogy to sanity-check the idea.
```

### Bad

```text
Gravity seemed normal.
```

### Better Direction

```text
Have the character notice an ordinary falling object, time it, reconstruct the forgotten equation, get an expected acceleration, and realize the environment is normal but their body or perception is wrong.
```

## Crisis Humor

Humor should appear as pressure release, not sitcom interruption.

Good crisis humor:

- exposes fear
- keeps the character moving
- contrasts technical competence with absurd circumstances
- appears in short bursts
- does not erase danger

Avoid humor that makes the crisis feel fake.

## Competence Without Perfection

The character may be competent, but not omniscient.

Show:

- forgotten formula recovered through derivation
- wrong assumption corrected by measurement
- rough estimate before precise answer
- safety margin chosen because the character is not completely sure
- improvisation with available materials
- embarrassment, irritation, fatigue, panic, or pettiness under the competence

## Science Explanation Gate

Before including a technical explanation, ask:

1. What is the immediate problem?
2. Why does the character need to explain or calculate this now?
3. What observation triggered the reasoning?
4. What action changes because of the conclusion?
5. What uncertainty remains?
6. What human emotion leaks through the explanation?

If the answer to 4 is “nothing changes,” cut or compress the explanation.

## Unit and Detail Discipline

Use numbers when they create decision pressure.

Good uses:

- a temperature threshold that determines failure order
- a mass that makes movement impossible
- a distance that affects timing
- a pressure reading that changes risk
- an acceleration that reveals an anomaly
- a material property that changes engineering choice

Weak uses:

- random numbers to sound smart
- long spec lists with no decision
- jargon that the POV would not actually think about

## Argument Voice

Technical explanation can also appear as argument.

Use when a character needs to convince someone:

- a plan is risky but rational
- a rescue is morally necessary
- an apparent impossibility is physically possible
- an alien signal or advanced knowledge may damage scientific culture
- a shortcut has hidden cost
- a tool will fail in a specific order

Keep the argument attached to stakes, not abstract debate.

## First-Person Texture

When the POV is first person, preserve personality:

- irritation at constraints
- petty pride
- fear disguised as jokes
- practical selfishness
- sudden self-awareness
- “I know this is stupid, but here is why I am doing it” logic
- confession that sounds casual but reveals desperation

Do not make the narrator sound like a clean technical manual.

## Final Checklist

Before presenting prose, verify:

- [ ] The technical explanation is caused by a scene problem.
- [ ] The explanation changes an action, decision, risk, or emotional beat.
- [ ] The POV has a reason to know or derive the information.
- [ ] Any numbers or material facts create pressure, not decoration.
- [ ] Humor does not erase danger.
- [ ] Competence includes uncertainty, cost, or fallibility.
- [ ] The voice still sounds like a person under pressure, not a textbook.
- [ ] No specific passage, distinctive phrasing, franchise lore, or copied scene structure is used.

## Principle

Science in fiction should not sit beside the scene. It should be the tool the character grabs while the room is on fire.
