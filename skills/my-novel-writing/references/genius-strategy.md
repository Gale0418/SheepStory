# Genius Strategy

## Purpose

This reference helps write intelligent characters without fake genius, enemy stupidity, or perfect plans.

Use it for:

- strategists
- detectives
- scientists
- inventors
- masterminds
- prodigies
- tacticians
- political operators
- high-IQ antagonists
- genius-vs-genius conflicts

## Rule 1: Intelligence Is Not Narration

Do not portray intelligence by merely saying:

- 他早已看穿一切
- 他微微一笑
- 她的計畫完美無缺
- no one could understand his genius

Show intelligence through observable work:

- noticing what others missed
- asking a better question
- rejecting the obvious plan
- choosing a costly tradeoff
- setting a fallback
- updating a model after failure
- forcing the opponent into a bad choice

## Genius Character Pass

Before drafting a genius scene, define:

```markdown
### Domain of Intelligence
What is this character specifically good at?
What are they bad at?

### Hidden Observation
What detail do they notice that others miss?

### Reasoning Chain
What steps connect observation to conclusion?
Can a careful reader reconstruct it later?

### Option Set
Plan A:
Plan B:
Plan C:
Rejected obvious plan:
Chosen plan:
Why this plan is best under constraints:

### Cost and Blind Spot
What does the plan cost?
What does the character underestimate?
Who is harmed, alienated, alerted, or sacrificed?
```

## Resource-Constrained Genius Check

Do not give genius characters perfect resources.

Before drafting a strategy scene, define:

```markdown
### Available Resources
- People:
- Money:
- Tools:
- Information:
- Time:
- Political cover:
- Terrain / environment:
- Public opinion:

### Resource Defects
For each resource, identify what is wrong with it:
- unreliable
- weak
- incomplete
- morally costly
- traceable
- slow
- politically dangerous
- emotionally unstable

### Strategic Conversion
How can each bad resource still be useful?
Can it become bait, noise, time, cover, leverage, or a sacrificial piece?

### Best Available Plan
What does the plan gain?
What does it risk?
What does it sacrifice?
What can go wrong?
What fallback exists?
```

## Adversarial Genius Check

When both sides are smart, do not only reverse-engineer the protagonist's win. Red-team the opponent.

```markdown
### Protagonist Model
- Goal:
- Known information:
- Hidden information:
- Resources:
- Constraints:
- Unacceptable cost:
- Blind spot:

### Opponent Model
- Goal:
- Known information:
- Hidden information:
- Resources:
- Constraints:
- Unacceptable cost:
- Blind spot:

### Red-Team Pass
For each protagonist plan:
1. How would a competent opponent detect it?
2. How would they counter it?
3. What would they protect first?
4. What false assumption could they reasonably make?
5. What cost prevents them from choosing the perfect counter?
```

A genius victory must come from at least one:

- asymmetric information
- forced tradeoff
- timing pressure
- moral constraint
- political constraint
- prepared fallback
- deliberate sacrifice
- opponent protecting the wrong priority for a rational reason

Never use:

- the enemy simply did not notice
- sudden enemy stupidity
- unexplained perfect prediction
- lucky timing without setup
- post-hoc reveal with no planted evidence

## Battlefield Anomaly Rule

A tactical genius may fail. The key is that failure updates their model.

Before executing a plan, define:

```markdown
### Expected Enemy Behavior
What does the protagonist think the enemy will do?

### Hidden Anomaly
What exists outside the protagonist's model?
Examples: ace pilot, unknown ability, betrayal, environmental failure, civilian interference, bad intelligence.

### Model Failure
Which assumption breaks first?
How does the protagonist detect that the model is wrong?

### Damage Control
Do they adapt, retreat, sacrifice pieces, or change objectives?

### After-Action Update
What do they learn?
How does future planning change?
```

## Twist Budget for Strategy Scenes

Do not stack genius reveals endlessly.

A strategic twist must have:

1. setup before reveal
2. cost after reveal
3. permanent state change
4. consequence scene after the twist

If a chapter contains multiple major reversals, justify why the compression is needed.

## Principle

A genius is not someone with a perfect answer. A genius is someone who can find the least bad answer faster, under worse constraints, and pay the cost knowingly.
