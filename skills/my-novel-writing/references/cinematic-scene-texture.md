# Cinematic Scene Texture

## Purpose

This reference helps the skill create prose with concrete scene texture, visual continuity, and lived-in worldbuilding.

The target is not to copy any specific copyrighted book, franchise, translation, setting, institution, or plot. The target is a general technique:

```text
Let the world enter through the character's body, objects, habits, friction, view, and desire.
```

## Core Rule

Worldbuilding must enter through scene texture, not external lecture.

Do not step outside the story to explain the world like an encyclopedia. Let the reader infer the world from what the character touches, sees, uses, wants, cannot afford, fears, misunderstands, or resents.

## Preferred Movement

Use a close-to-wide movement when opening or grounding a scene:

```text
body → object → room → window / corridor → city / battlefield / ship → distant scale → character desire
```

Examples of anchors:

- waking up and checking time
- shoes, clothing, scars, tools, medication, weapon maintenance
- a cramped table, a broken chair, a humming appliance
- a window, hatch, periscope, HUD, scope, telescope, elevator door
- street noise, corridor announcements, ship vibration, pressure seals
- a distant skyline, starport, battlefield, orbital ring, burning horizon
- the thing the character wants but cannot reach

## Scene Texture Plan

Before drafting a scene that needs strong atmosphere or worldbuilding, define:

```markdown
### Scene Texture Plan

- Close physical anchor:
- Character object:
- Daily friction:
- Immediate room / vehicle / street detail:
- Window, doorway, screen, scope, or vista:
- Wider world detail revealed through action:
- Sensory layer:
- Emotional filter:
- What the character wants from this place:
- What this place denies them:
- Which world detail changes action, mood, or decision:
```

## Embedded Worldbuilding

Avoid:

```text
The city used a three-tier transit system designed by the Central Planning Authority after the reforms of 2314.
```

Prefer a scene-functional direction:

```text
She missed the lower tram because the lift spent forty seconds arguing with her ration band. By the time the doors opened, the upper lanes had already swallowed the morning crowd, and the station ceiling was flashing the same polite apology in six languages.
```

The second version shows:

- transit hierarchy
- ration / identity infrastructure
- crowd density
- automated civic language
- character friction

without leaving the scene.

## Do Not Use Foundation-Style Inserts Unless Requested

Do not insert story-external encyclopedia passages, historical excerpts, policy documents, technical manuals, or institutional summaries unless the user explicitly asks for that structure.

If the story needs an epigraph, excerpt, or document fragment, it must have a dramatic reason:

- irony against the following scene
- false official version of events
- clue or contradiction
- institutional voice that affects the character
- setup for later consequence

Otherwise, keep the world inside the scene.

## Detail Selection

Good details do at least two jobs.

Prefer details that reveal:

- character class, age, profession, or status
- resource scarcity or abundance
- technology as habit, cost, or inconvenience
- political pressure through daily life
- emotional longing
- social surveillance
- military readiness
- decay, repair, or maintenance burden
- what the character considers normal

Weak details only decorate.

## Sensory Layer

Use more than sight, but do not overload every paragraph.

Possible layers:

- sound: ventilation, engines, distant traffic, drones, boots, alarms
- touch: cold floor, pressure suit seals, fabric, vibration, stale air
- smell: ozone, recycled air, detergent, metal, rain, oil, cafeteria steam
- light: screen glow, dawn, warning strips, orbital glare, reflected city lights
- motion: elevator sway, ship spin, traffic flow, crowd pressure

## POV Filter

Every description should be filtered through the character.

Ask:

- What does this character notice first?
- What do they ignore because it is normal?
- What irritates them?
- What do they envy?
- What do they long for?
- What detail hurts because of memory?
- What detail would another character describe differently?

## Anti-Exposition Checks

Flag and revise if:

- a paragraph could be pasted into a wiki page
- the narrator explains a system before the character interacts with it
- the world detail does not alter action, mood, choice, or tension
- the scene pauses for a history lesson
- the description floats without a body, object, or viewpoint
- the setting is impressive but emotionally neutral

## Scale Shift

For cinematic scale, move between near and far:

```text
small object → personal habit → room constraint → city / ship / battlefield system → distant horizon → private desire
```

The far scale should mean something to the character. A starport, orbital station, fleet, tower, or fortress is not just scenery; it should represent longing, threat, confinement, duty, class boundary, or impossible distance.

## Military / Hard-SF Texture

For military or hard-SF scenes, avoid dumping specs first.

Show technology through:

- maintenance routines
- checklists under pressure
- crew shorthand
- damage control
- ammo, fuel, heat, oxygen, latency, recoil, signal delay
- chain-of-command friction
- what the equipment cannot do
- what the character trusts too much

A machine becomes vivid when it limits the character.

## Final Checklist

Before presenting prose, verify:

- [ ] The scene begins from a body, object, or active situation, not a lecture.
- [ ] At least one world detail is revealed through action or friction.
- [ ] The description is filtered through the POV character.
- [ ] The setting affects mood, choice, tension, or desire.
- [ ] There is at least one concrete visual anchor.
- [ ] There is at least one sensory layer beyond generic sight when appropriate.
- [ ] No story-external encyclopedia insert appears unless explicitly requested.
- [ ] The prose uses detail density without stopping the scene.

## Principle

Do not tell the reader the world is vast. Let the character stand in a small room, touch one ordinary object, look out, and want the unreachable thing beyond the horizon.
