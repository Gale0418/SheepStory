# Style Profile: To-the-Stars-Inspired

## Purpose

This profile captures high-level traits inspired by the public Chinese translation/archive of `liuli-moe/to-the-stars` without copying its prose or imitating it line-by-line.

Use this profile when the user wants fiction with a feeling similar to:

- vast science-fiction scale
- rationalist / institutional worldbuilding
- alien social anthropology
- technical specificity
- quiet emotional pressure under policy, history, and survival
- human-scale wonder inside post-scarcity or posthuman systems

## Copyright Boundary

Do not copy sentences, names, chapter-specific inventions, alien terminology, or distinctive formatting from the source text.

Borrow only abstract craft principles:

- documentary framing
- systemic worldbuilding
- precise environmental detail
- intelligent characters reasoning under constraints
- emotional restraint
- awe filtered through technical and social context

## Core Feel

The prose should feel like:

```text
a calm observer walking through civilization-scale machinery, noticing that every system has moral weight.
```

The tone is not bombastic space opera. It is controlled, curious, analytical, and quietly vast.

## Structural Traits

### 1. Documentary Layer

Open or frame scenes with artifacts when useful:

- article excerpt
- research note
- policy memo
- security classification note
- historical quotation
- archival fragment
- glossary entry
- treaty clause
- mission report
- cultural annotation

These fragments should create context, irony, or moral pressure. Do not use them as decoration only.

### 2. Macro-to-Micro Transition

Move from large systems into personal experience:

```text
civilization-scale question
↓
institutional or technological implication
↓
local environment
↓
one character's body, attention, discomfort, curiosity, or fear
```

This lets the story feel enormous without losing the person inside it.

### 3. Technical Specificity

Use concrete numbers, constraints, and mechanisms when they affect the scene:

- temperature
- pressure
- time delay
- altitude
- signal range
- biological limits
- political clearance
- cognitive bandwidth
- energy cost
- translation uncertainty

Do not add numbers as decoration. A technical detail should change what the character can do, notice, fear, or misunderstand.

### 4. Alien Anthropology

When writing nonhuman societies, avoid making them humans with hats.

Track:

- body plan
- sensory priorities
- kinship model
- resource assumptions
- privacy norms
- risk tolerance
- communication channel
- what they find polite, rude, obvious, or sacred
- what they misunderstand about humans

Let alien kindness still feel alien.

### 5. Soft Emotion Under Hard Systems

Characters may speak calmly while the scene carries enormous stakes.

Prefer:

- restrained grief
- careful curiosity
- private awe
- bureaucratic horror
- tenderness mediated by technology
- moral discomfort expressed through observation

Avoid melodramatic explanation unless the POV or genre calls for it.

## Sentence and Paragraph Rhythm

Use a mix of:

- long explanatory sentences for systems and constraints
- medium sentences for movement and observation
- short sentences for realization, discomfort, or understated emotional impact

Do not make every paragraph symmetrical.

## Dialogue Style

Dialogue often carries:

- information asymmetry
- politeness with hidden risk
- translation uncertainty
- professional restraint
- questions used as probes
- cultural mismatch
- technical clarification that changes social meaning

Avoid pure exposition. Each speaker should still want something.

## Worldbuilding Rule

Every worldbuilding detail should do at least one job:

- constrain action
- reveal ideology
- create ethical tension
- show alien priorities
- change reader interpretation
- create future continuity obligation
- contrast with a character's expectation

If a detail does none of these, cut it.

## Anti-Pastiche Rules

Do not:

- copy the source's fictional names or alien markers
- reproduce source-specific document headers
- use fake complexity without story function
- flood the prose with terminology before the reader has a human anchor
- make every scene begin with a document fragment
- replace emotion with encyclopedia prose
- confuse rational tone with emotional emptiness

## Drafting Checklist

Before drafting in this profile, define:

```markdown
### System Layer
What civilization-scale system or institution presses on the scene?

### Local Constraint
What physical, political, biological, or informational limit shapes the action?

### Human Anchor
What does the POV character want, fear, misunderstand, or notice?

### Alien / Systemic Difference
What does another culture, machine, institution, or social model treat differently from the POV's expectations?

### Emotional Undercurrent
What feeling is present but not directly announced?

### Continuity Obligation
What fact introduced here must matter later?
```

## Revision Checklist

- [ ] The scene has a human anchor, not only system explanation.
- [ ] Technical details constrain action or perception.
- [ ] Alien or institutional behavior has internal logic.
- [ ] Emotional pressure is present but not over-explained.
- [ ] Macro-scale worldbuilding transitions into micro-scale embodiment.
- [ ] No copied source phrasing, names, or formatting.
- [ ] No fake jargon without consequence.

## Principle

Aim for awe with footnotes, tenderness under policy, and wonder that survives contact with bureaucracy.
