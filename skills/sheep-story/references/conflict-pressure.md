# Conflict Pressure

## Purpose

Conflict pressure prevents fiction from becoming too polite, too peaceful, or too quickly resolved.

AI often makes characters communicate like healthy adults. That can be pleasant, but it kills drama unless the peace has a purpose.

## Scene Requirements

At scene level, answer:

- Desire: What does someone want?
- Obstacle: Who or what resists it?
- Cost: What becomes worse, harder, or more revealing?
- Turn or function: What changes, settles, deepens, or becomes newly visible?
- Consequence: What carries into the next scene?
- Subtext: What matters without being said directly?

Individual beats do not each need all six properties. Each meaningful beat should advance at least one of action, information, relationship, emotion, risk, or persistent state. Reserve irreversible change for scene, sequence, or chapter turns where it fits.

## No Peaceful Scene Unless Earned

A peaceful or quiet scene is valid when it creates at least one of these:

- contrast before danger
- dread under apparent calm
- intimacy that raises later stakes
- false safety
- setup for betrayal, loss, or reversal
- emotional debt
- relationship texture that will later be tested
- recovery that reveals damage or changed priorities
- reflection that changes interpretation or choice

Do not manufacture conflict merely because a scene is calm. Also do not let characters resolve major conflict without earning the emotional and practical consequences.

## Active Pressure Menu

Ensure every scene has meaningful movement, consequence, or changed interpretation. Use an active pressure only when it fits the scene's established conflict; peaceful, reflective, recovery, and quiet scenes do not need manufactured tension.

Possible pressures include:

- someone wants something they cannot ask for directly
- someone hides something
- someone misunderstands something
- someone makes the situation worse
- someone refuses the healthy option
- someone pays a cost to keep control
- someone receives information they should not have
- someone chooses pride, fear, loyalty, guilt, or desire over reason
- a public role conflicts with a private need
- a tactical goal conflicts with an emotional need

## Dialogue Pressure

Dialogue should not be information exchange only.

For each major dialogue exchange, identify:

```markdown
Character A wants:
Character B blocks by:
What A says instead:
What A does not say:
What changes after the exchange:
```

## Conflict Smoothing Red Flags

Flag these:

- Everyone explains themselves clearly.
- The most reasonable solution is immediately accepted.
- The scene ends with no new problem.
- Characters apologize before the tension peaks.
- Secrets are revealed because the plot needs them revealed, not because pressure forces them out.
- The protagonist wins without losing leverage, trust, resources, or time.

## Revision Direction

When a scene is too peaceful, do not automatically add violence. Instead add:

- delayed confession
- incompatible goals
- social consequence
- strategic cost
- misread motive
- pride
- incomplete information
- external deadline
- witness or audience pressure

## Principle

A scene can be quiet. It cannot be inert.
