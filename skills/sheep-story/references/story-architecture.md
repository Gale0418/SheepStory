# Story Architecture

## Purpose

Use architecture when planning a complete short story, a multi-chapter arc, a major ending, or another unit whose overall causal shape matters. Skip formal architecture for bounded scene work when a compact outline is enough.

Architecture is a diagnostic map, not a formula. Select the lightest model that exposes the story's pressure, choices, and consequences.

## Inputs

Before selecting a model, identify:

- the approved Story Foundation or a sufficiently specific standalone brief
- the Project Brief when one exists, using its approved requirements and treating proposals as proposed
- the protagonist's private motive, flaw, and capability ceiling
- the core dramatic question
- the main source of opposition
- the target form and approximate scale, when supplied
- the promises and questions the ending must address

If one missing answer would produce fundamentally different stories, ask one blocking question. Otherwise label the choice proposed and continue to the approval gate.

## Select a Structure Model

Choose and briefly justify one model. Hybridize only when the combination clarifies the story.

### Causal Arc

Use when one disruption causes escalating choices, a crisis, and a consequence. This is the default for many compact stories.

### Four-Act Pressure Arc

Use when the story benefits from a distinct setup/break, active contest, severe reversal, and final decision. Four-act structure is optional, not mandatory, and act lengths follow dramatic function rather than fixed word percentages.

### Rise / Fall / Reckoning

Use for ambition, corruption, tragedy, obsession, or success that creates its own cost.

### Episodic Escalation

Use when repeated encounters change meaning, relationship, or pressure. Each episode must alter the next; repetition alone is not structure.

### Mosaic / Convergence

Use for multiple viewpoints, timelines, or fragments that accumulate toward a shared recognition or collision. Track what each strand contributes that the others cannot.

## Pacing By Function

Allocate space according to work performed:

- compress travel, repetition, and already-understood procedure
- expand difficult choices, model updates, reversals, and consequences
- place turns when prior causes have earned them
- use approximate word targets only when the user or publication format needs them

Do not require a climax every fixed number of words, fixed act percentages, a dialogue ratio, or a numeric payoff rate. These proxies can reward mechanical writing while hiding weak causality.

## Ending Check

A complete ending does not need to explain everything. It does need to:

1. answer the core dramatic question through a choice, failure, refusal, or consequence
2. show the price and resulting state
3. account honestly for governing promises
4. distinguish intentional ambiguity from a missing payoff
5. use opening-ending resonance only when the echo changes or deepens meaning

Opening-ending resonance is optional. Never force a mirrored line, image, or object merely to make the story look complete.

## Principle

Structure should reveal causality and cost, not make every story march to the same drum.
